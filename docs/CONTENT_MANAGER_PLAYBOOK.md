# Content Manager Playbook

This playbook turns the daily AI news workflow into a repeatable content system that is less likely to publish thin, generic, or low-trust content.

## Content goal

Build an audience that trusts us to filter AI news, explain what matters, and add a point of view that is useful to builders, operators, and AI-curious professionals.

## Audience

- Builders: people shipping with AI tools, agents, and models
- Operators: founders, marketers, consultants, and product leads following AI shifts
- Learners: professionals trying to keep up with AI without drowning in noise

## What strong content should do

- Filter signal from noise
- Add an original takeaway, not just summarize the headline
- Make the implication clear for a specific audience
- Attribute the source clearly
- Be formatted natively for the channel

## Editorial standards

- Every piece must answer: what changed, why it matters, and who should care
- Every piece must add one original takeaway or point of view
- Every sensitive or surprising claim should be checked against the original source and, when needed, a second trusted source
- Do not imply certainty where the source is speculative
- Do not publish just because a topic is trending if it is not useful to the audience
- Run the daily QA report before publishing so weak hooks, weak CTAs, and repeated angles get fixed while there is still time
- When the QA report flags a draft, use the generated backup hooks and CTA options instead of freehand patching under time pressure

## Content pillars

- Product shifts: launches, releases, new tools, model upgrades
- Research shifts: reasoning, benchmarks, papers, capability jumps
- Business shifts: funding, acquisitions, partnerships, go-to-market changes
- Policy shifts: regulation, safety, legal, compliance
- Workflow shifts: practical ways people can use the news in work

## Brand voice modes

The generator supports five voice modes through `AI_CONTENT_VOICE`:

- `creator`: sharper, more human, more publish-ready by default
- `operator`: practical, strategic, decision-useful
- `founder`: sharper, market-aware, advantage-focused
- `educator`: clearer, more explanatory, lower hype
- `newsroom`: concise, attribution-forward, tighter language

## Channel rules

### Newsletter

- Use short subject lines that scan on mobile
- A/B test subject lines when possible
- Measure clicks and replies, not only opens
- Lead with one strong story, then curate the rest

### LinkedIn

- Treat posts as conversation starters, not just announcements
- Prefer native formats like document, image, or video over plain link-only posts when possible
- Respond to engagement quickly
- Join other relevant conversations so distribution is not only dependent on your own post

### Instagram

- Build carousels for saves and shares, not only likes
- Keep each slide focused on one idea with strong first-slide packaging
- Use Reels with an immediate hook and captions for silent viewing
- Treat captions as support for the visual, not the whole post

### TikTok and Reels

- Land the hook in the first 3 to 6 seconds
- Keep one clear idea per clip
- Use captions because many viewers watch with sound off
- Keep the close clear so viewers know what to think, do, or watch next

### YouTube Shorts

- Keep the video vertical
- Use captions and text treatments to help people watching on mute
- Structure content clearly: hook, body, close
- Use Shorts as a discovery format, then move deeper analysis into longer posts, newsletter, or site content

## Daily workflow

1. Run the morning digest
2. Open the daily posting brief first
3. Review the quality report and fix weak hooks, soft CTAs, or repeated angles
4. Pick one lead story for video and one for the newsletter lead
5. Add your original take to each draft
6. Use the lead story to create a carousel, talking-head script, and X thread when the topic is strong enough
7. Publish natively for each channel
8. Monitor early engagement and reply fast
9. Log what angle won: product, research, business, policy, or workflow

## Weekly review

- Which stories earned the best click-through rate?
- Which videos held attention in the first 3 seconds?
- Which post angles drove comments, saves, and reposts?
- Which sources consistently produced strong-performing stories?
- Which topics got attention but weak conversion?
- Which formats took too much effort for too little return?

## KPI stack

- Newsletter: click-through rate, reply rate, unsubscribe rate
- LinkedIn: comments, reposts, saves, profile visits
- Short-form video: 3-second hold rate, average view duration, completion rate
- Site or landing page: clicks, scroll depth, conversions
